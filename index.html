Glory and Decline in Pittsburgh

History in 18th century

Europeans created what became the modern city of Pittsburgh after arriving around 1710.

Geography played a key role in the city's development because it sits at the confluence of the Allegheny, Monongahela and Ohio rivers, which made a perfect spot to develop a trading post.

Trappers and traders flocked to Pittsburgh, as did farmers and whiskey-makers.

Coal Changes the Economy

Pittsburgh's abundant natural resources along with the development of railroads, created favorable conditions for the rapid growth of the steel industry.

19th century

In the 19th century, the discovery of vast coalfields propelled Pittsburgh into a period of unprecedented success.

Pittsburgh Population

This map shows the population and population change in Pittsburgh from 1940 to 2010.

In the 40s and 50s, the population in Pittsburgh is still rising, but after the 50s, Pittsburgh has been losing its population.

That's because Pittsburgh has gone through a period from strength to weakness.

The iron and steel industry brought great wealth to Pittsburgh. But the overfocus on this one area became a weakness when the price of steel dropped. In the mid-20th century, the city began to lose ground.

American companies began to import steel from other places, such as Japan and Western Europe, which accelerated the end of the steel industry's development cycle. By 1970, 75% of the corporations headquartered in Pittsburgh were gone, leaving 110,000 steelworkers out of work. Pittsburgh was washed away overnight.

From Strength to Weakness

From 1976 to 1986, the number of manufacturing jobs fell by 45%, from 252k to 137k. From 1986 to 1996, the number of manufacturing jobs fell by 13%, from 137k to 119k. At that time, the employment rate in Pittsburgh was up to 20%.

Proportion of Industry

The histogram shows the changes in the share of different industries' employment in Pittsburgh from 1970 to 1990.

The share of manufacturing (durable manufacturing and nondurable manufacturing) declined significantly. The share of financial service, health service, and education increased significantly.

After the transition, by the 1990s, education and health care replaced the steel industry as the leading industry in Pittsburgh, and the steel industry is no longer in the top 10 industries.

The urban spatial structure also appeared to transform from "city" to "metropolitan areas". At that time, the city center reached the saturation stage after hundreds of years of development. Facilities and buildings were old, resulting in the relocation of manufacturing to suburbs and the "hollowing out" of the city center.

Between 1950 and 1990, Pittsburgh lost 45.3% of its population, from 677k (ranked 12th in the U.S.) to 370k (ranked 40th in the U.S.).

But on the contrary, in the 1950s, the population in Pittsburgh MSA was 2213k, in the 1990s, it decreased to 2395k, which indicated an 8.2% rise.

Revitalization

The collapse of the U.S. steel industry marked the transformation of Pittsburgh, an unexpected renaissance came with the closing of the mills and mines.

The "deindustrialization" led to significant changes in Pittsburgh's industrial structure.

Decrease in manufacturing and increase in services. Increased technology content within manufacturing. Traditional industries giving way to high-tech industries.

Civilian Employed by Industry

When the Ben Franklin Partnership was introduced in 1984, Pennsylvania's manufacturing employment rate had fallen by 21% and the unemployment rate had reached 14.9%, nearly twice the U.S. average.

The Ben Franklin Partnership sought to leverage Pennsylvania's strong educational resources to combine private enterprise with university research to increase competitiveness and develop new marketable technologies.

In this place where top-blown furnaces once spewed fire and smoke, through the collaboration with Carnegie Mellon University and the University of Pittsburgh, the Ben Franklin Technology Center has been established, which became an incubator for high-technology inventions.

Pittsburgh's high-tech industries, especially metal production, chemical and plastic production, biomedical technology, environmental technology, and information and communication products and services have developed one after another, and more than 150 industrial research institutions are established.

There are also robotics research centers in Pittsburgh, which made the Wall Street Journal graphically called "Roboburgh".

Location Quotient (LQ) is used to measure the proportion of employment in a specific industry in a specific region relative to the number of employment in that industry in the whole country.

Our radar chart visualization reveals the degree of specialization within Pittsburgh's 11 advanced industry clusters in 2015. The chart illustrates that Automation and Industrial Machinery, with a location quotient of 2.61, was the most specialized sector.

In the downtown "Golden Triangle" area, 23 of the U.S. top 500 companies set up their headquarters there. In recent years, Pittsburgh has become second only to New York and Chicago in the number of large corporate headquarters in the United States.

Company & Cultural District

This map showcases the top-performing companies across various industries in modern-day Pittsburgh. We have compiled and categorized these companies, and by hovering your mouse over the circles, you can see the company name and basic information about each one.

In the 21st century, Pittsburgh has been transformed into a thriving business and industrial city known for biotechnology, computer technology, robotics, health care, finance, and education. The "former Steel City" has once again been revitalized as a model of successful economic transformation for American cities.

Pittsburgh's transformation from an industrial hub to a city renowned for its cultural richness is emblematic of its deindustrialization process. The 14-block Cultural District, once a derelict industrial area, now stands as a testament to the city's successful transition. Conceived by H. J. Heinz II and managed by the Pittsburgh Cultural Trust, this district highlights the city's strategic shift from heavy industry to arts, culture, and service sectors. This successful re-purposing of urban spaces represents Pittsburgh's innovative response to deindustrialization, embracing a sustainable future without disregarding its industrial past.

U.S. AirQuality 1985-2022

Historically, Pittsburgh's heavy industry brought both economic gains and environmental issues. From 1985 to 1995, the city saw worsening air quality, culminating in a peak AQI of 221. However, post-2005, stricter emission regulations from the revised Clean Air Act led to consistent air quality improvements. By 2020, good air quality days reached 218 with a significantly lower maximum AQI of 128. Pittsburgh's progress highlights the potential for environmental improvement through policy enforcement, technological advancement, and societal commitment.

Co2 Emission

Our research focuses on the carbon emissions from transportation in Pittsburgh between 2018 and 2021. Despite improved air quality, carbon emissions from transportation remain a challenge. Traffic flow entering and leaving the city contributes more to carbon emissions than traffic flow within the city. We analyzed carbon emissions data at three locations: "inbound," "outbound," and "in boundary."

By understanding the contribution of traffic flow at these locations to overall carbon emissions, we can develop more accurate carbon emission reduction strategies. From 2018 to 2021, carbon emissions at these locations have decreased, indicating positive effects from the deindustrialization and urbanization processes.

Emissions by Economic Sector

Pennsylvania's CO2 emissions across different sectors have changed from 1990 to 2020, reflecting the state's economic transformations. The industrial sector, which previously characterized Pittsburgh's economy, peaked in emissions in 2018 at 80.60 MMT CO2 equivalent, but by 2020, it had reduced to 76.88 MMT CO2 equivalent. However, the Electric Power Industry substantially decreased its emissions from 131.09 MMT CO2 equivalent in 2007 to 73.01 MMT CO2 equivalent in 2020. The commercial and residential sectors showed relatively stable emission trends. Overall, while deindustrialization led to a decrease in CO2 emissions, the transition to a service-based economy poses environmental challenges. Sustainable practices across all sectors are necessary for reducing overall emissions as Pittsburgh continues to evolve.
